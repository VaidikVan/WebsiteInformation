Hummingbird

Feeding

All hummingbirds are nectarivorous and are the most specialized of
bird species. Hummingbirds have numerous adaptations to nectarivory,
including long, probing bills and tongues which rapidly take up fluids.
Hummingbirds also posses the most advanced and sophisticated
hovering flight of all birds. Their intestines are capable of extracting
over 99% of the glucose from nectar feedings within minutes, owing to
high densities of glucose transporters (the highest known among
vertebrates)

Adaptations

One of the hummingbirds most useful adaptiations for feeding are their
beaks. The extreme curved beaks of sicklebills are adapted for extracting
nectar from the curved corolla tubes of Centropogon flowers. Some
species, such as hermits (Phaethornis spp.), have long beaks that enable
insertion deeply into flowers with long corolla tubes. Thornbills have
short, sharp beaks adapted for feeding from flowers with short corolla
tubes and piercing the bases of longer ones. The beak of the fiery-tailed
awlbill has an upturned tip adapted for feeding on nectar from tubular
flowers while hovering.

Another useful adaptation that the hummingbird uses for feeding is
their specialized tounge. Hummingbirds drink with their long tongues
by rapidly lapping nectar. Their tongues have semicircular tubes which
run down their lengths to facilitate nectar consumption via rapid
pumping in and out of the nectar. While capillary action was believed to
be what drew nectar into these tubes, high-speed photography revealed
that the tubes open down their sides as the tongue goes into the nectar,
and then close around the nectar, trapping it so it can be pulled back into
the beak over a period of 14 milliseconds per lick at a rate of up to 20
licks per second. The tongue, which is forked, is compressed until it
reaches nectar, then the tongue springs open. This allows them to unload
nectar via a pump.

Other adaptations include their:

  • Wingbeats and flight stability
  • Perception of sweet nectar
  • Visual cues of foraging
  • Feeders and artificial nectar
